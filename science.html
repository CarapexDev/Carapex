Research dossier

The Science Behind InfinityCoat(TM)

Published studies suggest that chitin nanofiber films can form dense, low-permeability structures with useful processing latitude. InfinityCoat(TM) is Carapex's program to evaluate whether that literature-backed materials logic can translate into practical pharmaceutical coatings.

The evidence below distinguishes what has been reported in packaging, biomaterials, and chitosan-derivative coating literature from what Carapex is still testing on dosage-form surfaces, release behavior, and manufacturing fit.

Barrier performance potential

Solution-cast chitin nanofiber films were reported at roughly 1.9-2.4 cm^3*um/m^2/day/kPa oxygen permeability, which the authors described as about 5x lower than oriented PET and in the same order of magnitude as high-barrier PVDC. This is strong barrier evidence, but it is not yet a tablet-coating result. [Lit. 1]

Sustainable feedstock

Chitin is a bio-based structural polysaccharide commonly sourced from shell waste streams, and the cited chitin nanofiber work is centered on water-processed fibrillation routes. That supports renewable feedstock potential, not a full Carapex life-cycle claim. [Inference from Lit. 1-3]

Manufacturing compatibility

Partial deacetylation reduced homogenization demand from 30 passes to 8 and lowered required pressure by 63% while retaining low oxygen permeability in the reported film study. That suggests processability headroom worth testing in coating workflows. [Lit. 1]

Why chitin

Why chitin nanofibers matter for barrier films

The working hypothesis is straightforward: fibrillated chitin can create a thin, dense network that makes diffusion paths more tortuous while remaining processable in water-based systems. The literature does not prove InfinityCoat(TM), but it does provide a technically credible rationale for evaluating chitin-derived coatings.

Dense fibril packing can lower gas transmission

In barrier-film studies, homogenized chitin nanofibers formed transparent films with low reported oxygen permeability. The likely mechanism is a compact, high-aspect-ratio network that limits diffusion through the film thickness rather than relying on heavy inorganic fillers. [Lit. 1]

Wet-state performance can be reinforced

Chitin nanofibril composite literature shows that supramolecular cross-linking strategies can improve swollen-state modulus, strength, and oxygen barrier relative to PVA alone. This matters because oral coatings must perform after exposure to humidity and process water, even though the cited system is not itself a tablet coat. [Lit. 2]

Chitin chemistry is relevant to active-containing systems

Separate chitin scaffold work demonstrates that natural chitin matrices can absorb and release actives through retained capillary structures. That is not oral dosage evidence, but it does support the broader view that chitin-based materials can participate in controlled mass transfer rather than acting as inert shells only. [Lit. 3]

Coating precedent exists in chitosan derivatives

Enteric tablet studies using chitosan succinate and chitosan phthalate show that derivative choice and blend design materially affect acid protection and alkaline release. This provides formulation precedent for chitin-derived coating families while underscoring that chemistry selection matters. [Lit. 4-5]

Research pillars

Research pillars that will shape the dossier

These pillars are not repeats of the mechanism story above. They are the five program lenses Carapex should use to decide whether the literature is strong enough, transferable enough, and practical enough to justify deeper coating development.

1. Transferability to dosage-form geometry

The first question is whether flat-film barrier results can survive the transition to curved, defect-sensitive tablet and capsule surfaces. Literature on low oxygen permeability is necessary, but Carapex still has to prove uniform coverage, edge integrity, and acceptable coat continuity on real dosage forms. [Lit. 1]

2. Environmental robustness under storage stress

A coating candidate is only useful if it remains intact through humidity exposure, handling, and storage. The humid-condition composite literature is therefore a screening lens for robustness, helping define what kinds of reinforcement or formulation controls may be needed before product testing. [Lit. 2]

3. Compatibility with active and release requirements

Chitin-based materials cannot be assessed as barrier layers alone; they also have to avoid unacceptable interactions with release profiles or active migration. The scaffold and enteric studies together make this a formulation-screening lens rather than a simple barrier question. [Lit. 3-5]

4. Chemistry selection and formulation discipline

The enteric coating papers show that small chemistry changes can decide whether a coating system succeeds or fails. This pillar is therefore about narrowing polymer state, derivatization, blend strategy, and solids design instead of assuming that any chitin-derived material will behave adequately. [Lit. 4-5]

5. Manufacturing feasibility before scale claims

Process burden matters early. The homogenization-efficiency study makes manufacturability a decision criterion in its own right by showing that fibrillation energy can change materially with pretreatment while barrier performance remains promising. Carapex still needs direct coating-process evidence before claiming scale compatibility. [Lit. 1]

Research library

Curated literature library

Each entry below includes a concise takeaway, key findings, a note on how directly it supports InfinityCoat(TM), and the full staged PDF for review.

[Lit. 1]

Increasing efficiency of the homogenization process for production of chitin nanofibers for barrier film applications

Takeaway: This paper provides the clearest direct rationale for evaluating chitin nanofibers as barrier layers because it ties low oxygen permeability to a more practical fibrillation process window.

  • Reported oxygen permeability remained low at roughly 1.9-2.4 cm^3*um/m^2/day/kPa across the tested film variants.
  • The authors compared that level to about 5x lower than oriented PET and similar in magnitude to high-barrier PVDC.
  • Partial deacetylation reduced homogenization from 30 passes to 8 and lowered pressure demand by 63% while retaining low oxygen permeability.

Support type: Direct support for barrier-film potential and processability inference. This remains a solution-cast film study, not a pharmaceutical tablet-coating study.

[Lit. 2]

Supramolecular cross-linking affords chitin nanofibril nanocomposites with high strength and water resistance

Takeaway: This study supports the idea that chitin-based barrier systems can be engineered for humid or swollen conditions without giving up transparency and oxygen-barrier relevance.

  • Swollen-state modulus reached up to 3.0 GPa in the reported nanocomposite system.
  • Tensile strength reached up to 70 MPa while visible transmittance stayed above 70%.
  • Oxygen permeability improved relative to PVA alone, indicating that the chitin nanofibril network contributed barrier value in the composite.

Support type: Indirect support for humid-condition mechanics and barrier design. The system includes PVA and tannic acid in a packaging-film context rather than an oral coating formulation.

[Lit. 3]

Naturally Drug-Loaded Chitin: Isolation and Applications

Takeaway: This paper is useful because it shows that native chitin architectures can absorb and release actives, which is relevant to mass-transfer thinking even though it is not a dosage-form coating study.

  • The isolated natural chitin scaffold retained a capillary network rather than collapsing into a featureless matrix.
  • The scaffold absorbed and released actives in the reported assay format.
  • Antimicrobial inhibition zones of 6 mm and 4 mm were reported for the naturally drug-loaded samples.

Support type: Indirect support for chitin matrix interaction with active transport. It does not demonstrate oral dosage performance, tablet coating behavior, or InfinityCoat(TM) efficacy.

[Lit. 4]

Preparation and Characterization of Chitosan Succinate as Coating Polymer for Enteric-Coated Tablet

Takeaway: This paper is a useful pharmaceutical precedent because it shows that chitosan-derived coating systems can approach enteric performance when blended appropriately, but the derivative alone was not enough.

  • Chitosan succinate plus HPMCP blends met enteric criteria in acid more effectively than chitosan succinate alone.
  • Successful blends reported acid-stage release values up to 8.53% and 6.74%.
  • Release in alkaline medium exceeded 80%, indicating recovery of downstream dissolution in the reported blends.

Support type: Direct support for chitosan-derived enteric coating precedent, with an important caveat: this is derivative and co-blend evidence, not proof of InfinityCoat(TM) or chitin nanofiber coatings.

[Lit. 5]

Evaluation of chitosan succinate and chitosan phthalate as enteric coating polymers for diclofenac sodium tablets

Takeaway: This study is important mainly because it shows that derivatization choice can decide whether delayed-release specifications are met.

  • Chitosan phthalate met USP delayed-release specifications in the reported diclofenac tablet study.
  • Chitosan succinate did not meet the same delayed-release target under the tested conditions.
  • A 2.5% loading was noted for the successful chitosan phthalate coat.

Support type: Direct support for formulation-selection discipline in chitosan-derived coatings. It is not Carapex-specific evidence and does not establish chitin nanofiber barrier performance by itself.

Validation status

What is proven vs what we are testing

The current evidence base is enough to justify experimental work, but not enough to claim product performance. Carapex treats the items below as separate categories on purpose.

Published literature supports

  • Chitin nanofiber films can deliver low oxygen permeability in barrier-film studies. [Lit. 1]
  • Properly designed chitin-based composites can retain useful wet-state mechanics and improved oxygen barrier. [Lit. 2]
  • Chitin matrices can absorb and release actives in non-oral scaffold systems. [Lit. 3]
  • Chitosan-derived coatings can achieve delayed-release performance in selected enteric formulations. [Lit. 4-5]

Carapex validation in progress

  • Whether InfinityCoat(TM) can reproduce a meaningful oxygen and moisture barrier on tablets or capsules.
  • How the coating behaves during pan or fluid-bed processing, drying, adhesion, and defect control.
  • Whether the system preserves required dissolution or delayed-release performance for target products.
  • How coating thickness, substrate type, storage conditions, and scale-up affect reproducibility.

Interpretation standard

On this page, literature-backed means reported by external authors in the cited studies. Under evaluation by Carapex means internal validation work is still required before any product-level claim should be made.
