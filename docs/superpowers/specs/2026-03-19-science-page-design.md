# Carapex Science Page Design

## Summary

Design a new `Science` page within the existing Carapex site that explains the research basis for InfinityCoat(TM) in a technically credible, lab-forward format. The page should feel like a deep-tech science dossier rather than a homepage or investor pitch deck. It should combine custom visuals, concise explanatory copy, explicit literature citations, and five embedded research PDFs.

This page is not the primary homepage. It is a supporting page within the site, intended to strengthen credibility with investors, technical partners, judges, and grant reviewers without overclaiming Carapex-specific validation.

## Goals

- Add a dedicated `Science` page to the existing site navigation.
- Present the scientific rationale behind InfinityCoat(TM) in a way that is readable for non-specialists but still defensible to technical readers.
- Curate five strongest source papers for the page body and embed their PDFs on-page.
- Separate published-literature support from Carapex's own ongoing validation work.
- Introduce a few custom visuals so the page does not read like a paper dump.

## Non-Goals

- Replacing the current homepage.
- Turning the page into a full academic publication archive.
- Claiming Carapex clinical, commercial, or validated product performance that has not yet been proven internally.
- Embedding every PDF the user shared.

## Audience

The page should serve multiple audiences at once:

- Investors looking for a more serious technical basis behind the company story.
- Technical partners, CDMOs, and pharma manufacturers evaluating whether the concept is scientifically plausible.
- Competition judges and grant reviewers who expect explicit evidence and citations.

The writing should assume mixed literacy: technically informed, but not written as a journal article.

## Page Positioning

- Nav label: `Science`
- Page title: `The Science Behind InfinityCoat(TM)`
- Tone: lab-forward, precise, restrained, and credible
- Visual model: `Science Dossier`

The page should read like a structured technical explainer with evidence, not a commercial brochure and not a research poster.

## Content Guardrails

- Use exact paper titles or clean citation labels rather than raw filenames.
- Distinguish clearly between:
  - `Published literature suggests`
  - `Carapex is testing`
  - `Carapex target / estimate`
- Avoid presenting literature values as if they are Carapex-generated data.
- Avoid unqualified product claims such as `30% better shelf life` unless explicitly labeled as an internal estimate or target.
- Avoid implying regulatory, clinical, or customer validation that does not yet exist.
- Keep claims tethered to what the cited paper actually supports.

## Page Architecture

### 1. Hero

Purpose:
Introduce the page as the scientific basis for InfinityCoat(TM), not as a sales page.

Content:
- Page title: `The Science Behind InfinityCoat(TM)`
- Short intro paragraph explaining that Carapex is building a chitin-based pharmaceutical coating informed by published work on nanofiber films, barrier behavior, processability, and coating-related material performance.
- One concise sub-line stating that the page combines literature-backed rationale with areas currently under Carapex validation.

### 2. Evidence Strip

Purpose:
Provide fast, scannable proof points near the top of the page.

Format:
Three compact evidence blocks, each clearly labeled as literature-backed rationale rather than final product proof.

Recommended blocks:
- `Barrier Performance Potential`
- `Sustainable Feedstock`
- `Manufacturing Compatibility`

Each block should use short technical copy and, where possible, a literature-linked quantitative callout.

### 3. Why Chitin Works

Purpose:
Explain the material logic in plain technical language.

Core topics:
- Dense chitin nanofiber film structure
- Relevance of oxygen and moisture barrier behavior
- Film transparency, strength, and tunability
- Why those properties matter for tablets and capsules
- Why sustainably sourced chitin is strategically different from fossil-derived coating polymers

This section should prioritize mechanism and material logic, not hype.

### 4. InfinityCoat(TM) vs Conventional Coating Logic

Purpose:
Give readers a comparison framework without overstating product maturity.

Format:
A table or matrix comparing:
- `Conventional fossil-derived coating systems`
- `Published chitin-film literature`
- `Carapex validation in progress`

Suggested rows:
- Material source
- Oxygen barrier relevance
- Moisture barrier relevance
- Film strength / integrity
- Process compatibility
- Sustainability profile
- Validation status

The third column must explicitly signal in-progress validation rather than completed proof.

### 5. Research Pillars

Purpose:
Break the science into digestible themes with visuals and concise copy.

Format:
Four or five pillar cards with a small custom visual cue, headline, and short text.

Recommended pillars:
- Oxygen barrier
- Moisture resistance / wet stability
- Film strength and transparency
- Processability and manufacturability
- Coating / drug-interface relevance

These cards should help bridge technical readers into the curated papers below.

### 6. Curated Research Library

Purpose:
Present the strongest five references in a structured, readable way.

Format for each paper:
- Formal title
- Short one-sentence takeaway
- Two or three key findings
- Clear note on what the paper supports directly
- PDF embed directly below or adjacent to the summary block

Curated five papers:

1. `Increasing efficiency of the homogenization process for production of chitin nanofibers for barrier film applications`
Use for:
- strongest barrier-film/processability support
- low oxygen permeability story
- reduced homogenization burden

2. `Supramolecular cross-linking affords chitin nanofibril nanocomposites with high strength and water resistance`
Use for:
- wet-strength and water-resistance support
- supportive barrier context under humid conditions
- note clearly that the system includes PVA and tannic acid, not pure chitin alone

3. `Naturally Drug-Loaded Chitin: Isolation and Applications`
Use for:
- related evidence that chitin matrices can interact with and release actives
- supportive material relevance, not direct pill-coating proof

4. `Preparation and Characterization of Chitosan Succinate as Coating Polymer for Enteric-Coated Tablet`
Use for:
- related evidence that chitosan-derived coatings can be formulated for tablet-coating applications
- note clearly that this is derivative/co-blend evidence, not InfinityCoat(TM) proof

5. `Evaluation of chitosan succinate and chitosan phthalate as enteric coating polymers for diclofenac sodium tablets`
Use for:
- related enteric-coating precedent
- highlight that derivative choice matters and not all chitosan-based systems perform equally

### 7. What Is Proven vs What We Are Testing

Purpose:
Protect credibility and make the maturity of the work legible.

Format:
A two-column or split-panel section.

Left column: `Supported by Published Literature`
- Chitin/chitosan-derived films can exhibit strong barrier-related and film-property advantages in relevant contexts.
- Chitosan-derived systems have precedent in coating or drug-delivery applications.
- Processing and material properties can be tuned meaningfully.

Right column: `Currently Being Tested by Carapex`
- Oxygen and moisture diffusion coefficients
- Transparency
- Drug release properties
- Performance in a pharma-specific coating context
- Integration into real manufacturing workflows and partner validation

This section should explicitly state that Carapex is in early-stage development.

### 8. References

Purpose:
Provide transparent citation support without cluttering the main narrative.

Format:
- Full references for the curated five
- Secondary references listed below in lighter form, without needing embeds

Secondary references may include papers on chitosan film tuning, protonation chemistry, process kinetics, and other internal-background sources, but they should not dominate the page.

## Visual Direction

The page should include original visuals, but keep them technical and restrained.

Required visual types:

- `Mechanism graphic`
  Show a tablet or capsule wrapped by a dense chitin nanofiber barrier layer, with oxygen and moisture pathways visually reduced.

- `Evidence metrics band`
  Three to four literature-labeled metric callouts, such as oxygen permeability, process-efficiency gains, transparency, or wet-strength context.

- `Comparison matrix`
  A structured comparison between conventional coating logic, literature-supported chitin potential, and Carapex validation status.

- `Research cards`
  Five visually consistent paper modules for the curated library.

- `Validation status panel`
  A clear literature-vs-Carapex split.

Visuals should support comprehension and credibility, not decorative marketing.

## Writing Style

- Short, dense paragraphs.
- Scientific language where useful, but avoid sounding like a manuscript.
- Minimize vague adjectives like `revolutionary`, `groundbreaking`, or `industry-leading` unless directly tied to evidence and carefully qualified.
- Prefer precise phrasing such as `published studies suggest`, `reported in literature`, or `currently under evaluation by Carapex`.

## Information Hierarchy

Priority order:

1. Explain why the material is promising.
2. Show what published literature actually supports.
3. Show how that maps to InfinityCoat(TM).
4. Clarify what Carapex is still validating.
5. Offer embedded primary sources for deeper review.

Readers should be able to stop at any point and still leave with an accurate understanding.

## Open Implementation Decisions

These should be resolved during planning and implementation, not by changing the page intent:

- Whether the science page becomes a dedicated `science.html` page or a sectioned route pattern within the current static site structure.
- Exact nav placement and whether the page is linked from the header only or also from other sections.
- Final citation formatting style.
- Exact responsive treatment for multiple embedded PDFs on mobile.

## Success Criteria

The page is successful if:

- It feels more technical and lab-forward than the homepage.
- It remains readable for mixed audiences.
- It includes five curated embedded PDFs and explicit citations.
- It uses visuals to clarify the science rather than replace it.
- It avoids overclaiming Carapex-specific results.
- A reviewer can clearly tell what is literature-backed and what is still being tested.
